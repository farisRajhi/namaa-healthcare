# Saudi Patient Behavioral & Cultural Patterns

> **ALWAYS LOADED** — These cross-cutting patterns apply to every clinic type and every campaign. They override generic best practices whenever there is a conflict.

---

## Financial Calendar & Salary Cycle

### Salary Day Effect (25th-27th of each month)
- Most Saudi employees (government and private sector) receive salaries on the **25th-27th** of each Gregorian month.
- **Peak booking window**: 25th through the 3rd of the following month. Patients are most willing to book and pay during this window.
- **Campaign timing**: promotional offers and paid-service reminders perform 30-40% better when sent on the 25th-26th vs mid-month.
- **Avoid**: sending payment-required messages on the 15th-20th (lowest wallet confidence).

### Insurance Renewal Cycle
- Many employer insurance policies renew in January. Q4 (Oct-Dec) is "use your benefits" season.
- Patients with expiring benefits are highly receptive to checkup and screening campaigns.

## Islamic Calendar & Religious Seasons

### Ramadan (رمضان)
- **Clinic hours shift**: most clinics operate split shifts — closed during the day, open from after Iftar (~7pm) until midnight or later.
- **Pre-Ramadan rush** (2 weeks before): dental cleanings, diabetic consultations, general checkups.
- **During Ramadan**: lower elective visit volume, higher urgent care for fasting-related issues (hypoglycemia, dehydration, headaches, gastric distress).
- **Cosmetic rush**: skincare treatments and minor cosmetic procedures spike in the last 10 days of Ramadan as patients prepare for Eid al-Fitr.
- **Campaign rule**: reduce outreach frequency during Ramadan. Focus messages in the evening (after Iftar, 8-10pm).

### Eid al-Fitr (عيد الفطر)
- **1 week before**: cosmetic and grooming appointments peak. Hair, skin, dental whitening.
- **During Eid** (3-5 days): clinics are closed or minimal. Do not send campaigns.
- **Post-Eid**: return to normal within 1 week. Good time for "new season, new health goals" messaging.

### Eid al-Adha (عيد الأضحى)
- **4 weeks before**: cosmetic preparation begins (similar to Eid al-Fitr but slightly lower volume).
- **During Eid** (4-5 days): clinics closed. No campaigns.
- **Post-Eid**: some clinics see minor injury cases (related to Eid activities). Regular campaigns resume after 1 week.

### Hajj Season
- If the clinic is in Makkah/Madinah, expect surge volume from pilgrims.
- For non-Hajj-city clinics, many staff and patients travel — expect lower volume.
- Travel health checkups and vaccinations (meningococcal) are relevant 1-2 months before.

## Weekly & Daily Rhythms

### Weekend & Work Week
- **Weekend**: Friday (الجمعة) and Saturday (السبت).
- **Sunday** (الأحد) is the first business day — highest appointment demand.
- **Thursday**: many patients try to squeeze in appointments before the weekend.
- **Do not send campaigns on Friday** — it is the holy day and families are together. Saturday is acceptable for non-urgent messages.

### Prayer Times (أوقات الصلاة)
- Five daily prayers, times shift by ~1-2 minutes each day and vary significantly by season.
- **Absolutely do not send messages during prayer times** — this is considered disrespectful and will damage the clinic's reputation.
- Approximate windows to avoid (these shift seasonally):
  - Fajr: ~4:30-5:15am (summer) / ~5:15-6:00am (winter)
  - Dhuhr: ~12:00-12:30pm
  - Asr: ~3:15-3:45pm (winter) / ~3:30-4:00pm (summer)
  - Maghrib: ~5:45-6:15pm (winter) / ~6:45-7:15pm (summer)
  - Isha: ~7:15-7:45pm (winter) / ~8:30-9:00pm (summer)
- **Safe send windows**: 9:00-11:30am and 4:15-5:30pm are the most reliable year-round.

## Seasonal Behavioral Patterns

### Summer (Jun-Aug)
- **Wedding season**: huge demand for cosmetic, dental whitening, dermatology.
- **Travel checkups**: families traveling abroad want health clearances, vaccination records.
- **School's out**: pediatric routine visits drop, but injury visits may increase.
- **Peak AC season**: respiratory issues from extreme temperature shifts (50°C outside → 18°C inside).

### Back-to-School (August)
- **School health certificates**: required for enrollment, includes vision, hearing, vaccination status.
- **Campaign opportunity**: target families with school-age children in early-mid August.
- **Vaccination catch-up**: parents realize missing doses when reviewing school requirements.

### Saudi National Day (September 23)
- Public holiday. Some clinics run promotional offers.
- Patriotic-themed campaigns are acceptable and can perform well if done tastefully.
- Not a major medical event, but a good engagement opportunity.

## Communication & Channel Preferences

### WhatsApp Dominance
- **~95% of Saudi patients** prefer WhatsApp over SMS or email for clinic communication.
- WhatsApp messages have 85-95% open rates vs 20-30% for SMS in Saudi Arabia.
- Patients expect to be able to **reply** to WhatsApp messages and get a response.
- Voice notes on WhatsApp are popular — some patients will send voice notes instead of typing.

### Language & Dialect
- **Arabic-first**: always lead with Arabic. English can be secondary for expat-heavy clinics.
- **Gulf Arabic** (اللهجة الخليجية): use familiar greetings — مرحبا، أهلاً وسهلاً، السلام عليكم.
- Avoid Egyptian or Levantine dialect markers — Saudi patients notice and it feels foreign.
- **Formal vs friendly**: medical/clinical messages should be formal (نود تذكيركم بموعدكم — "We would like to remind you of your appointment"). Cosmetic/wellness can be friendlier (أهلاً! وقت تجديد بشرتك — "Hi! Time to refresh your skin").

### Trust Signals
- **Always mention the clinic name** in messages — patients receive many promotional messages and need to identify the sender.
- **Mention the doctor's name** when relevant — Saudi patients often choose a specific doctor, not just a clinic.
- **Avoid generic templates** — personalization (patient name + specific service) dramatically increases response rates.

## Family Dynamics in Healthcare Decisions

- **Father**: often the financial decision-maker and insurance holder. Payment-related messages may need to reach him.
- **Mother**: typically the healthcare scheduler for children and often for elderly parents. Appointment reminders and preventive care campaigns should target her.
- **Adult children**: increasingly schedule and pay for elderly parents' care.
- **Family packages**: offering family checkup bundles resonates strongly. "Book for the whole family" performs better than individual offers.

## Booking Behavior

- **Minimize friction**: include a direct booking link in every message. Every extra step loses ~30% of potential bookings.
- **"Reply to book" pattern**: "أرسل 1 للحجز" ("Send 1 to book") converts well on WhatsApp because it requires zero effort.
- **Preferred booking method**: WhatsApp > phone call > website > app. Align the CTA to the channel you are messaging on.
- **Social proof**: mentioning that the clinic is busy or popular builds trust, but **never fabricate** numbers or reviews. Use real data like "Dr. Ahmad has 3 slots remaining this week" if available.
- **No-show culture**: no-show rates in Saudi clinics can reach 25-35%. Confirmation messages 24h and 2h before the appointment significantly reduce this.
